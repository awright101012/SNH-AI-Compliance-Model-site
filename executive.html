Compliance AI, Built Around Your Rules

A company-specific compliance engine that reasons from your guide, explains its decisions, and can become a private fine-tuned SLM in days.

SNH Strategic InitiativesA Wright OriginalResearch brief · July 2026

Executive Summary

  • Every screening company applies compliance differently. Statutes establish the outer boundary, but companies still make operating choices about evidence, identity, dispositions, timing, exclusions, review thresholds, and customer-specific procedures. A generic model cannot safely guess those choices.
  • We built an AI compliance engine that begins with the company’s own guide. An SLM with a harness reads the relevant rulebook, reasons through each criminal case in the guide’s required order, explains the decision, cites the controlling policy, and routes unresolved evidence to a reviewer.
  • We can then turn that behavior into a private, company-specific model. A fine-tuned SLM learns the decision pattern from synthetic examples, allowing the organization to operate with compact open-source weights under its own control rather than sending case data to a third-party model provider.
  • The feasibility result is already material. In a retrospective program covering 24,641 searches and 82,543 court cases, the governed SLM and the fine-tuned SLM both demonstrated that small models can perform complex criminal-record adjudication at operational scale. The first working system was assembled in roughly a week; company-specific model iterations can be produced in days once the guide and evaluation contract are ready.

The real problem is that compliance is both legal and operational

Background-screening decisions are not produced by law alone. Two companies can read the same legal landscape and implement different, counsel-approved operating procedures. They may use different identity thresholds, interpret the same disposition vocabulary differently, apply different customer requirements, or choose different points at which uncertainty becomes manual review.

That means the product cannot be one universal table of answers. It must be a system that can faithfully apply the rulebook it has been authorized to follow.

Human adjudicators already work this way. They read the company guide, inspect the available case facts, follow the decision sequence, resolve exceptions, document the rationale, and stop when the evidence is insufficient. The opportunity is not to replace that judgment with generic text generation. It is to make the same disciplined workflow executable, measurable, and repeatable.

What we built

The platform has two complementary forms.

SLM with a harness

The SLM with a harness works open-book. For every case, it receives the normalized evidence and only the portions of the company guide that may control the decision. Deterministic safeguards own facts and boundaries that must be exact. The SLM handles the remaining interpretation. An independent verifier checks whether the answer is complete, supported, and consistent with the supplied guide.

The output is more than a label. It is a decision record containing:

  • the case facts that mattered;
  • the policy path that controlled;
  • the decision and reviewer posture;
  • a plain-language explanation;
  • citations back to the company guide; and
  • a trace that can be replayed after the rulebook or model changes.

If the evidence or reasoning does not satisfy the contract, the system does not invent certainty. It routes the case to manual review with the unresolved issue preserved.

Fine-tuned SLM

The fine-tuned SLM is the private, company-specific form. Instead of receiving rule text at runtime, it learns the organization’s adjudication pattern from synthetic examples generated from the approved guide. It learns how the company sequences the decision, which facts matter, when to abstain, and how to explain the result.

This creates a practical deployment option for organizations that want model inference inside their own environment. The demonstrated 9B-class model can be served on a single 24 GB workstation or laptop GPU. Keeping the model local can keep normalized case inference away from external AI providers, subject to the organization’s surrounding data and hosting controls.

The two forms solve different operating needs. The SLM with a harness is easier to update when a rule changes and offers the clearest live provenance. The fine-tuned SLM is compact, private, and fast to deploy at scale. They can also work together: the harness remains the control architecture while the fine-tuned SLM becomes its private reasoner.

How a new company becomes a new compliance engine

The reusable asset is not one customer’s answer set. It is the conversion process.

  1. Define the operating authority. Identify the company guide, counsel-approved interpretations, customer overlays, and review policy that the system is permitted to apply.
  2. Turn prose into a decision contract. Make precedence, exceptions, required evidence, and abstention conditions explicit enough to test.
  3. Normalize the case evidence. Convert inconsistent source records into stable facts while preserving missingness and warnings.
  4. Build the governed engine. Give the SLM the relevant guide sections, constrain its output, verify its claims, and preserve the trace.
  5. Generate synthetic training cases. Create controlled examples that cover ordinary decisions, boundary conditions, contradictions, and required manual review without using real people as training material.
  6. Fine-tune the private SLM. Teach a compact open-source model to reproduce the approved decision behavior, then test it against held-out cases it did not see during training.
  7. Deploy with measured authority. Start with reviewer assistance, measure errors and abstentions, and expand automation only where the evidence supports it.

The architecture is designed to repeat this process for different company guides. The current program proves the method on one detailed screening rulebook; a new company’s guide would still require its own mapping, counsel review, and certification.

Results that establish feasibility

The proof program exercised real operational complexity rather than a small set of demonstration prompts.

  • 24,641 searches and 82,543 court cases were adjudicated in the retrospective replay.
  • The SLM with a harness reached 96.1% handbook agreement on its final decisions in the clean model-under-test view, with deterministic decision guards disabled so the result measured the SLM’s reasoning rather than code-owned answers.
  • The strongest fine-tuned SLM reached 94.47% search-level exact agreement and 94.86% case-level accuracy against the decision engine on the full development replay.
  • The fine-tuned model produced structured decisions at scale, while the broader program demonstrated local, OpenAI-compatible serving on a single 24 GB GPU.
  • The first working rulebook-grounded and fine-tuned lanes were built in roughly a week, with later model iterations taking hours to days rather than months of application redevelopment.

These figures answer a feasibility question: can compact open-source AI learn to reason through complex criminal cases and reproduce a company’s approved compliance behavior? The answer is yes.

They do not mean that one model is universally correct for every company or rulebook. The results are retrospective, the guide is the evaluation authority, and each new implementation requires company-specific legal and operational validation.

What this gives a screening company

The immediate value is not simply lower model cost. It is control over the compliance system itself.

  • Company-specific behavior: the engine applies the organization’s approved guide rather than a provider’s generic policy assumptions.
  • Private deployment: open-source weights can run inside company-controlled infrastructure.
  • Faster policy change: the governed engine can update its rulebook without rebuilding an entire application, while the fine-tuned model can be refreshed from the new guide.
  • Reviewer leverage: analysts receive a decision, explanation, policy path, and clear reason for any abstention.
  • Auditable improvement: errors can be traced to evidence normalization, policy interpretation, model reasoning, or verification rather than disappearing inside one opaque score.
  • A path to bespoke models: each company can own a compact compliance SLM trained around its procedures and risk posture.

The next step

For a new organization, the right pilot is narrow and concrete: choose one rulebook, one record family, and one existing adjudication workflow. Convert the guide into a testable decision contract, run the SLM with a harness beside human reviewers, and use the approved traces to train a private fine-tuned SLM.

The measure of success is not whether the model sounds like a reviewer. It is whether it applies the authorized guide consistently, explains the controlling path, abstains for the right reasons, and improves against held-out evidence.

Evidence boundary

The architecture is intended to support different company guides, but the quantitative results above come from one retrospective background-screening program. Agreement is measured against the encoded operating guide and decision engine, not independent legal truth. Private deployment reduces the need to send normalized case data to third-party model providers only when the surrounding hosting, logging, and integration stack is also company-controlled. Operational authority remains subject to counsel approval and company-specific validation.
