SNH AI: Matched Comparison

A matched review of recall-side misses, handbook alignment, and explanation consistency.

SNH Strategic InitiativesA Wright OriginalResearch brief · July 2026

Matched comparison

The review compares recall-side misses, handbook alignment, and explanation consistency across the same retrospective screening program.

Matched recall-side result

The comparison workbook isolates 1,440 searches that UBS ultimately reported. A miss on this slice means the system would have cleared a search that the historical workflow reported.

System Recall-side misses Miss rate
SNH AI 93 6.5%
SLM with a harness 24 1.7%
Fine-tuned SLM 17 1.2%

The SLM with a harness produced roughly four times fewer recall-side misses than SNH AI. The fine-tuned SLM produced still fewer in this point-in-time comparison.

This is a positive-only recall slice, not an overall accuracy score. Every row was a historical positive, so the comparison measures missed positives rather than precision on the much larger population of clears.

The broader retrospective view

Across 24,641 vendor-hit searches containing 82,543 court cases, the reported comparison view showed the SLM with a harness at 96.1% agreement with the encoded handbook on its final decisions. SNH AI was at 61.5% in the deck’s matched rulebook view.

The denominators and defer rates differ, so those percentages should not be read as a universal head-to-head accuracy score. Their value is directional: a rulebook-grounded reasoner with explicit verification aligned more closely to the selected operating guide than the existing output path.

Why the architecture behaved differently

The newer system separated work that had previously been entangled:

normalize every case
→ select the current company rules
→ reason in procedural order
→ cite the controlling policy
→ verify the decision independently
→ aggregate cases safely
→ route unsupported conclusions to review

The advantage was not AI alone. It was the combination of bounded reasoning and deterministic control.

Internal consistency review

A separate audit examined 24,287 exported SNH AI rationale rows. It found 2,410 rows—9.9%—with at least one internal consistency issue. In those rows, the final answer, reasoning, parsed facts, or cited rule did not fully reconcile.

Examples of the recurring patterns included:

  • a final answer that conflicted with its own stated rule;
  • timing logic applied when the necessary dates were missing;
  • an exclusion applied without supporting evidence;
  • disposition language that did not match the parsed record; and
  • citations that appeared in the explanation without controlling the outcome.

Most high-confidence conflicts also overlapped a retired rule-template family or its related generic seven-year suppression pattern. That is evidence of textual and rule-family lineage. It is not proof of source-code provenance.

What the comparison does and does not establish

The comparison supports three narrow conclusions:

  1. On the same historical-positive slice, the SLM with a harness and the fine-tuned SLM produced fewer direct misses.
  2. A current, explicit rulebook plus independent verification reduced the risk that the explanation and final answer would point in different directions.
  3. The architecture could be assembled and iterated much faster than a policy path distributed across accumulated rules and templates.

It does not establish that one system is legally correct independent of the selected guide, that historical UBS outcomes are a perfect legal label, or that any model is authorized for autonomous production decisions. Those questions require company policy owners, counsel, held-out evidence, and an approved operating boundary.
