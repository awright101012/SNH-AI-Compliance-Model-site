A Private Compliance Model for Each Company

Compact open-source models can learn a company’s approved adjudication behavior and run inside company-controlled infrastructure.

SNH Strategic InitiativesA Wright OriginalResearch brief · July 2026

The same law does not produce the same operating guide at every screening company.

Compliance programs reflect legal advice, customer obligations, risk tolerance, data sources, review operations, and years of institutional decisions. The result is a company-specific way of turning a court record into an action.

That is why the future of compliance AI is unlikely to be one universal model making the same decision for every organization. A more credible future is a common architecture that can build a governed engine—and ultimately a private fine-tuned SLM—around each company’s approved procedures.

Company-specific does not mean improvised

A bespoke model should not learn policy by copying historical reviewer outcomes. Historical decisions may contain old rules, inconsistent handling, or customer exceptions that no longer apply. Real case rows also create unnecessary privacy and governance risk when used as training material.

The better sequence is:

  1. establish the current company guide as the operating authority;
  2. convert it into explicit decision and abstention behavior;
  3. test that behavior in an SLM with a harness;
  4. generate synthetic cases that exercise the guide’s ordinary and difficult boundaries; and
  5. fine-tune a compact model to reproduce the approved behavior.

This teaches the model what the organization intends to do, not merely what happened in the past.

What the fine-tuned SLM learns

The fine-tuned SLM is trained to do more than classify a record. It learns a structured adjudication discipline:

  • identify which facts are decisive;
  • follow the company’s required policy sequence;
  • distinguish convictions, non-convictions, pending matters, relief, and exclusions;
  • apply timing and jurisdiction rules only when their prerequisites are present;
  • explain how the facts connect to the outcome;
  • produce a stable machine-readable response; and
  • abstain when the evidence does not support a final decision.

The intended behavior resembles a trained reviewer using an operating guide: inspect the evidence, apply the relevant section, document the reasoning, and stop when a required fact is missing. The model is not a person and does not replace legal judgment; it reproduces a bounded adjudication process under measurable constraints.

Why small, open-source models matter

Compliance data is sensitive. Many organizations do not want identifiable case content sent to a general-purpose AI provider, retained in provider logs, or exposed to a changing external model.

A compact open-source SLM changes that deployment equation. The organization can control the model weights, serving environment, access policy, logs, upgrades, and retention behavior. The demonstrated 9B-class model fits a single 24 GB workstation or laptop GPU, making private inference possible without a large multi-GPU cluster.

Local deployment does not automatically create privacy. The surrounding application, storage, monitoring, and network controls must also remain inside the approved boundary. But it gives the company a realistic way to keep model inference under its own operational control.

Why a model can be built in days

Traditional compliance software often distributes policy across code branches, configuration, templates, and manual procedures. A policy change can require development, regression testing, and reconciliation across several systems.

The fine-tuning process starts from a different asset: a versioned decision contract and synthetic case generator. Once those are ready, the team can create a new curriculum, train a compact model, and run held-out evaluations in hours to days. The first complete rulebook-grounded and fine-tuned program was assembled in roughly a week.

The speed does not come from skipping rigor. It comes from reusing the architecture:

company guide
→ approved decision contract
→ synthetic boundary cases
→ fine-tuned SLM
→ held-out certification
→ private deployment

What changes for each customer is the policy and validation evidence. The model-building machinery remains stable.

Results from the demonstrated fine-tuned SLM

The strongest 9B fine-tuned SLM was evaluated against the company-specific decision engine across a retrospective development replay of 24,641 searches and 82,543 court cases.

  • 94.47% search-level exact agreement
  • 94.86% case-level accuracy
  • 91.63% case-level balanced accuracy
  • Structured adjudication across the full replay on compact open-source weights

Earlier iterations established the core feasibility quickly; later iterations improved the balance between decisive answers, unsafe disclosures, false exemptions, and manual review. That progression is part of the product: the model can be retrained against measured failure families without turning real case rows into training examples.

These are development results, not universal certification. The full retrospective set informed model improvement, so a customer deployment still needs a fresh or sealed holdout after the model and policy are frozen.

The SLM with a harness remains essential

The private model does not eliminate the governed architecture. The two systems protect different things.

The SLM with a harness keeps the current rulebook external, selected, and reviewable. It is the best place to prove a new guide, resolve conflicting instructions, and respond quickly to policy changes. The fine-tuned SLM packages that approved behavior into compact weights.

A practical operating pattern is therefore:

  • use the SLM with a harness as the policy control and evaluation authority;
  • use the fine-tuned SLM as the private company-specific reasoner;
  • keep exact safeguards and verification outside the model; and
  • retrain or roll back when the guide changes or held-out performance falls.

This makes the model replaceable without making the company’s compliance policy disposable.

What this could change for background screening

A company-specific fine-tuned SLM can become a shared compliance capability across operations:

  • first-pass adjudication of normalized criminal cases;
  • reviewer recommendations with explanations and policy citations;
  • consistent treatment across teams and shifts;
  • rapid testing of a proposed guide revision before rollout;
  • quality assurance focused on the cases where model and reviewer disagree;
  • private batch processing without per-case calls to a third-party AI model; and
  • a reusable foundation for additional record types after separate validation.

The product is not merely an automation tool. It is a way for a company to make its operating judgment explicit, testable, teachable, and deployable.

The standard for earned authority

A bespoke model should earn authority one role at a time. It can begin by organizing evidence and drafting recommendations. It can advance to reviewer-assist when its explanations and abstentions are reliable. Narrow automated decisions should come only after a sealed evaluation demonstrates that the model is safe on that exact population and the company has approved the operating boundary.

The goal is not maximum automation on day one. The goal is a private compliance engine that can become more useful without becoming less accountable.

Evidence boundary

The demonstrated fine-tuned SLM learned one company-specific screening guide. The architecture is intended to repeat the process for other guides, but each requires separate policy mapping, synthetic curriculum design, held-out evidence, and counsel approval. Reported accuracy is agreement with the encoded decision engine on a retrospective development replay, not independent legal truth or a production authorization.
